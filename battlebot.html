Overview

Fred is a compact competition robot focused on delivering high kinetic energy impacts to the underbellies of opponents. The design emphasizes energy transfer efficiency, quick serviceability, and aggressive strategy in bracket-style matches.

Performance Highlights

  • Peak spin speed: ~6,800 RPM (calcuated)
  • Approx. 3 lb (weight-legal)
  • Solid 1 lb weapon system which can deliver a max of roughly 125 joules of kinetic energy upon impact (calculated)
  • Robot is almost entirely 3D printed on an ender 3, and costs a total of about $300-350 to make fully

Build Details

Mechanical Design

Chassis & Structure

  • Low-profile 3D-printed chassis designed with pockets and "crumple zones" to isolate impacts
  • Aluminum front plate to block head on attacks and volley under opponents
  • Optimized mass distribution to lower center of mass, making the structure more stable and controllable

Weapon & Drive

  • Brushless 920kv motor serves as the weapon pulley
  • Custom motor mount made of petg, which distributes impact shock across the chassis and downwards into the wheels
  • Belt driven ack-wheel drive using high-torque DC gearmotors (Gecko v2 motors from Just cuz robotics)
  • Shore 30 Silicon molded wheels for high traction

Electronics & Controls

  • ESC + receiver for weapon control and drive control, using Radiolink transmitter/reciever, Weka 2.0 dual channel brushed ESC, and generic 20A brushless ESC
  • Battery: 4S LiPo (1500 mAh), offering higher voltage for the drive and weapon systems to operate much more aggressively
  • Wiring harness designed for quick field repairs

Weapon Testing & Iteration

Version 0

  • Weapon Motor and torque calculations were confirmed
  • Belt was insufficient and lacked proper strength
  • Belt mounts wore down quickly
  • Belt slippage decreased the effectiveness of energy transfer
  • need to fix weapon system

Version 1

  • Aluminum weapon pulley mounted to motor
  • larger reduction between motor and weapon pulleys
  • Spinner spun up to full speed and demonstrated consistent spin up time
  • Weapon vibrations shook the entire chassis and weapon should be realigned

Version 2

  • added more robust bearings for the weapon
  • fixed the "weapon wobble" by fixing the weapon mounting to the horizontal shaft more securely
  • Motor placement was changed to keep it better protected from potential impacts
  • Construction of weapon mounts distributes weight into the chassis rather than absorbing the majority of the impact

Driving Testing & Iteration

Version 0

  • determining belt material and size
  • initial belt was too thick and to short - caused too much tension between the wheels
  • wheel design began with plastic PLA circles with rubber bands glued on
  • Needed better belt and better wheels

Version 1

  • used a smaller polyurethane belt which could be crimped to the proper size
  • substituted rubber bands for TPU sleeves around wheels
  • TPU did not maintain traction sufficiently

Version 2

  • added bearings to the front wheels
  • used shore 30 silicon molded wheels to provide better traction
  • molds failed to demold properly
  • wheel speed was too slow

Version 3

  • fixed the wheel mold to allow easier demolding
  • upgraded battery from 3s lipo to 4s lipo for better speed

Chassis Testing & Iteration

Version 0

  • initial design was made of 3 main bodies of PLA (base, front, and back) as well as two PLA sideguards
  • Screws stripped inside the plastic, shape of body left no room for electronics
  • weapon was mounted inside the chassis

Version 1

  • Moved weapon forward to leave more space for electronics
  • added heat inserts for screws
  • Chassis shattered easily during impact testing

Version 2

  • Changed Chassis design entirely, leaving a gap in the middle and two side compartments to house electronics
  • New design employed PETG for weapon mounts and sideguards, but continued to use PLA for the main chassis
  • Impact testing revealed the chassis as a whole was more flexible and could adequately isolate impacts to reduce damage to the full system

Competitions:

  • Nov 1, 2025

    • About Competition:

  • Just about everything that could go wrong did go wrong. We showed up as a team the day before competition to pass safety inspection and the bot performed amazingly, with high ground control, speed, and damage. This was still true the day of the competitino, when we tested the bot in the pits 30 minutes before battle. Then, for some reason, our left drivetrain siezed up and would not operate properly, causing us to lose the initial advantage and made it quite difficult to deliver a hit. The chassis, which did infact isolate single impacts, did not do so well after being repeatedly struck. To make matters worse, the cage lights did not turn on after we tapped out of the competition, leading to our competitor delivering a crushing blow that shredded the entire right side of our bot, leaving it in a state beyond simple repair. We back to the pits to get it fixed, as we only had an hour before our next competition. There was a blur of commands as I dished out tasks to my teamates, to make sure we could have the robot operating in time. While our drill did die, making it impossible to set two important screws in, we did manage to make a functional, though not pretty version of FRED rise up from the scraps. It was missing an inner side, and the reciever was taped in a wad of electrical tape to the top end of the robot, it appeared to be in suitable condition. Especially because somehow the drivetrain was working better than before. We were then forced to forfeit our second match because, in our frantic repair session, the signal wire to our weapon motor had accidentally been stripped out.
