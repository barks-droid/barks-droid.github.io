Overview

Fred is a compact competition robot focused on delivering high kinetic energy impacts to the underbellies of opponents. The design emphasizes energy transfer efficiency, quick serviceability, and aggressive strategy in bracket-style matches.

Performance Highlights

  • Peak spin speed: ~6,800 RPM (calculated)
  • Approx. 3 lb (weight-legal)
  • Solid 1 lb weapon system delivering ~125 J of kinetic energy (calculated)
  • Almost entirely 3D printed on an Ender 3; total cost about $300–350

Build Details

Mechanical Design

  • Low-profile 3D-printed chassis with impact isolation zones
  • Aluminum front plate for deflection and wedging
  • Mass optimized for low center of gravity and high stability

Weapon & Drive

  • Brushless 920 kv motor powering the weapon via belt reduction
  • Custom PETG weapon mounts dispersing impact shock through chassis
  • Belt-driven, 4-wheel drive using Gecko v2 DC gearmotors
  • Shore 30 silicone-molded wheels for superior traction

Electronics & Controls

  • Radiolink TX/RX with dual ESC setup (Weka 2.0 + 20 A brushless)
  • 4S LiPo (1500 mAh) powering both systems for aggressive performance

Weapon Testing & Iteration

Version 0

  • Confirmed motor torque and spin-up calculations
  • Belt lacked strength and wore down quickly
  • Slippage reduced energy transfer efficiency

Version 1

  • Upgraded to aluminum weapon pulley with greater reduction between motor and weapon pulleys
  • Reached full spin speed consistently
  • Weapon vibration indicated mounting misalignment

Version 2

  • Added more robust bearings and improved weapon shaft coupling
  • Relocated weapon motor for improved impact protection and protection
  • Weapon mounts designed to distribute impact through the chassis

Driving Testing & Iteration

Version 0

  • Initial belts too thick and short, creating excess tension
  • Prototype wheels used rubber bands glued on PLA

Version 1

  • Replaced belt with smaller polyurethane belt whish could be crimped to the proper length
  • replaced rubber bands with TPU wheel sleeves, but they lacked traction

Version 2

  • Added bearings to front wheels
  • Adopted Shore 30 silicone molds for traction
  • mold was not made properly, causing demolding to be very difficult

Version 3

  • Fixed mold design for easy demolding
  • Upgraded to 4S LiPo for higher drive speed

Chassis Testing & Iteration

Version 0

  • nitial design was made of 3 main bodies of PLA (base, front, and back) as well as two PLA sideguards
  • Weapon was mounted inside the chassis, leaving very little room for electronics
  • screws easily stripped in the plastic

Version 1

  • Moved weapon forward in the chassis to leave more space, added heat inserts
  • PLA still shattered easily during impact

Version 2

  • Redesigned chassis into dual-compartment structure, with the weapon mounted in-between them
  • Mixed PETG/PLA build improved flexibility and impact isolation

Competitions:

  • Nov 1, 2025

    • About Competition:

    Just about everything that could go wrong did go wrong. The day before the competition, our team passed safety inspection with no issues. The bot performed exactly as intended, showing excellent ground control, speed, and striking power. Even on the morning of the event, when we tested it in the pits, everything was still operating perfectly. Then, during the loading for the first match, the left drivetrain suddenly seized. This eliminated our initial advantage and made it difficult to maneuver effectively or deliver clean hits. The chassis, which had been designed to isolate single impacts, began to deform under repeated strikes. After we tapped out of the match, the cage lights failed to activate, and our opponent delivered an additional hit that tore apart the entire right side of the robot, leaving it far beyond simple repair. We had only an hour to rebuild before our next match. The repair session quickly became a coordinated rush as I assigned tasks and tried to keep progress steady. Our drill failed midway through, preventing us from installing two critical screws, but we still managed to restore functionality. The rebuilt version wasn’t elegant—one side was missing, and the receiver was secured to the top of the frame with electrical tape—but the drivetrain actually performed better than before. Unfortunately, just before the next round, we discovered that the signal wire to the weapon motor had been stripped loose during repairs, forcing us to forfeit the match.
